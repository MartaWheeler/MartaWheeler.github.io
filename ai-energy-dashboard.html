AI's Power Bill — how data-center growth is reshaping U.S. electricity

After a decade of flat demand, U.S. electricity consumption broke trend right as generative AI infrastructure went into hyperscale buildout. This page walks through real public data — EIA grid data, Nvidia's reported Data Center revenue, and state electricity prices — to ask a simple question with a careful answer: is AI actually moving the needle on the grid, and if so, where?

Data: EIA API · Nvidia 10-Q/8-K filings Built with: Python · JavaScript · Chart.js Updated: —
01 · The Question

Electricity demand was flat for a decade. Then it wasn't.

For roughly fifteen years, U.S. electricity demand barely moved — efficiency gains roughly offset population and economic growth. Since 2023, that flat line has bent upward. The obvious suspect is the generative AI buildout: data centers are projected to grow from 31 GW of demand in 2025 to 66 GW by 2027, and that doesn't happen without showing up somewhere in the grid data. This page tests that story against the numbers, not just the headlines.

03 · Where It Shows Up

Not all grids are growing the same way

If AI data centers are really driving the trend, the regions with the heaviest data-center buildout — ERCOT (Texas) and PJM (Mid-Atlantic / Virginia) — should show steeper demand growth than regions without that concentration, like MISO and CISO (California). This is the closest thing to a natural experiment the public data allows.

04 · Controlling for Noise

Demand has always had rhythm — the question is whether the trend survives it

Electricity demand is deeply seasonal: daily (AC-driven afternoon peaks), weekly (lower on weekends), and yearly (summer/winter peaks). Before crediting any change to AI, it's worth showing the baseline noise explicitly. 05 · The Price Side

Virginia hosts the heaviest data-center concentration in the country — by some estimates, data centers consume roughly a quarter of the state's electricity. 07 · Being Honest About the Limits

What this analysis does and doesn't prove

Read before you cite this anywhere

This shows a correlated pattern, not isolated causation. Electricity demand growth since 2023 has several plausible drivers beyond AI data centers — EV adoption, reshoring of manufacturing, extreme weather years, and broader cloud/digitization trends all contribute. Public utility data does not separately tag "AI load" as its own category, so no public dataset can cleanly isolate it.

What the data does support: data-center buildout is a documented, material contributor to recent demand growth (EIA and multiple industry analysts treat it as significant), and the regions with heaviest data-center concentration are showing the patterns this analysis would predict if the AI story is real. That's a defensible, evidence-based pattern — not a definitive causal claim.

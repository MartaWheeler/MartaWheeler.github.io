The question this dashboard is testing ▸
For roughly fifteen years, U.S. electricity demand barely moved — efficiency gains roughly offset population and economic growth. Since 2023, that flat line has bent upward. The obvious suspect is the generative AI buildout: data centers are projected to grow from 31 GW of demand in 2025 to 66 GW by 2027. This dashboard tests that story against real public data, not just headlines — using EIA grid data, Nvidia's reported Data Center revenue as a proxy for AI compute buildout, and state-level electricity prices.
What do ERCOT, PJM, MISO, and CISO mean? ▸

These are grid operators — the organizations that manage electricity supply and demand for a specific region of the U.S. EIA reports demand data by grid operator, not by state, so these codes are how the underlying data is organized. Two were picked because of heavy AI data-center buildout, two as comparison regions:

ERCOT
Electric Reliability Council of Texas. Manages the grid for most of Texas. Included because Texas has one of the heaviest concentrations of new AI data-center construction in the country.
PJM
PJM Interconnection. Manages the grid across 13 Mid-Atlantic and Midwest states, including Virginia — which has the single highest concentration of data centers in the U.S. (~26% of the state's electricity). This is the "ground zero" region for the AI/grid story.
MISO
Midcontinent Independent System Operator. Manages the grid across 15 central states (Midwest/Great Plains). Included as a comparison region — less data-center buildout, showing what "normal" demand growth looks like without the AI effect.
CISO
California Independent System Operator. Manages California's grid. Also a comparison region — California has its own tech-industry presence, but a more mature data-center base and very different energy policy (heavy renewables mandates), so its demand is shaped by different forces.

What this analysis does and doesn't prove ▸

This shows a correlated pattern, not isolated causation. Electricity demand growth since 2023 has several plausible drivers beyond AI data centers — EV adoption, reshoring of manufacturing, extreme weather years, and broader cloud/digitization trends all contribute. Public utility data does not separately tag "AI load" as its own category, so no public dataset can cleanly isolate it.

What the data does support: data-center buildout is a documented, material contributor to recent demand growth, and the regions with heaviest data-center concentration show the patterns this analysis would predict if the AI story is real. That's a defensible, evidence-based pattern — not a definitive causal claim.
